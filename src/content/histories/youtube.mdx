## Design evolution analysis

YouTube began life looking like exactly what it was: a scrappy 2005 startup.
Early snapshots show a **light gray, table-based layout**, star ratings under
every video, and the unforgettable "Broadcast Yourself" tagline. The player was
small, the thumbnails were tiny, and the whole page assumed a slow connection.

After the 2006 Google acquisition, the design slowly professionalized. The
palette cooled, the player grew, and recommendations crept up the page. The real
turning point is the 2010–2013 era, when the layout widened to fill modern
monitors and the sidebar of recommended videos became the engine of "just one
more video" viewing.

## Major feature changes

- **2007 — Annotations & embedding:** YouTube videos start showing up all over
  the web, turning the site into infrastructure.
- **2010 — HTML5 player:** the slow migration off Flash begins, improving
  performance and battery life.
- **2015 — Mobile-first layout:** the grid becomes responsive cards that reflow
  cleanly from phone to desktop.
- **2017 — Material redesign:** rounded cards, a lighter chrome, and the modern
  red-play-button logo arrive together.

## UX changes

The clearest UX story is the rise of the **recommendation sidebar**. In 2006 you
mostly searched for videos; by 2016 the homepage *was* a recommendation feed.
Star ratings gave way to a simple like/dislike, then to likes only. The player
controls migrated from a cramped strip to a clean overlay, and autoplay turned
passive watching into the default behavior.

## What to look for in the timeline

Watch the **thumbnail grid** grow from tiny boxes to full-bleed cards, the
**player** expand and lose its Flash chrome, and the **tagline** quietly vanish
as "Broadcast Yourself" gave way to an algorithmic feed.
