## Design evolution analysis

Facebook’s earliest snapshots are startlingly plain. The 2004 "TheFacebook"
page is a **narrow column** with a deep blue header, a tiny logo featuring a
man’s silhouette, and copy explaining that the site was "an online directory
that connects people through social networks at colleges." It looked like a
student project — because it was.

The signature blue (#3b5998 in those years) became one of the most recognizable
colors on the web, chosen partly because Zuckerberg is red-green colorblind. For
nearly fifteen years that blue bar anchored the top of every page, until the
2019 "FB5" redesign finally pushed it aside for a bright, white, card-based look
borrowed from the mobile app.

## Major feature changes

- **2006 — News Feed:** the move from "visit profiles" to "the feed comes to
  you" redefined social media.
- **2008 — Tabbed profiles:** Wall, Info, and Photos tabs organized an
  ever-growing pile of content.
- **2011 — Timeline:** profiles became a visual autobiography with a cover photo.
- **2019 — FB5:** rounded corners, more whitespace, and a retreat from the blue
  chrome.

## UX changes

The throughline is a steady shift from **directory to feed to platform**. Early
Facebook expected you to browse profiles; modern Facebook assembles an infinite,
ranked stream. Navigation moved from text links to icons, the like button (2009)
turned engagement into a one-tap reflex, and reactions (2016) added nuance.
Each redesign also nudged more real estate toward content you didn’t choose to
see.

## What to look for in the timeline

Track the **blue header bar** (dominant for over a decade, then minimized), the
**profile layout** (single column → tabs → Timeline), and the steady growth of
**feed content** relative to navigation chrome.
